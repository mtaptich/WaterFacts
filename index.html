Your Water Footprint

Explained Visually

By Mike Taptich, Thomas Hendrickson, Kelly Archer, and Jennifer Stokes Draut

About 30 percent of the contiguous United States was in at least a moderate drought as of December 16. Conditions seem to be particularly bad in California. The state's last 36 months were the hottest and driest in the 119-year instramental record.

Accordingly, many water utilities have asked their customers to cut their water usage in an effort to conserve the state's limited resource. In the Bay Area, for instance, the East Bay Municipal Utility District has called for a 15% reduction in water usage, or about 37 gallons per day conserved. By best estimates, it will take at least months of above-normal rain to end this drought.



So, what can you do to reduce your water footprint in these notably dry times?

Well, you should begin your conservation efforts by breaking this particular question down into three major parts:

  1. How much water do I directly consume and use?
  2. How much water is embedded in the products I consume and use?
  3. How much water is embedded in the energy I use?

Though often forgotten, the last two questions are very important for truly reducing the total amount of water you use daily. It is easy to monitor the amount of water you directy consume and use. All you would need is a volumetric measuring device (e.g., a measuring cup) and you are good to go. However, for the last two questions, you need a more scientific approach for this accounting.

Water resource engineers and scientists will employ a or LCA to estimate the total water demand for a produce, service, or infrastructure system. LCAs are extremely time and data intesive, however offer many useful pieces of information for choosing between different alternatives.
